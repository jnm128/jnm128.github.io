Introduction

backround

The Philippine Islands became a Spanish colony during the 16th century and were ceded to the US in 1898 following the Spanish-American War. Led by Emilio Aguinaldo, the Filipinos conducted an insurgency against American rule from 1899-1902, with some fighting continuing until 1913.

In 1935, the Philippines became a self-governing commonwealth, with Manuel Quezon as president, preparing for independence after a 10-year transition. The islands fell under Japanese occupation in 1942 during World War II, but US forces and Filipinos regained control during 1944-45.

The Republic of the Philippines attained independence on Jul. 4, 1946. Ferdinand Marcos' 21-year rule ended in 1986 with a "people power" movement in Manila, EDSA 1, which installed Corazon Aquino as president, despite several coup attempts.

Fidel Ramos was elected president in 1992, bringing increased stability and economic reforms. Joseph Estrada was elected in 1998 but was succeeded by Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo in 2001 after his impeachment trial and a second 'people power' movement, EDSA 2.

Arroyo's term until 2004 saw the economy expand each year despite corruption allegations. Benigno Aquino III was succeeded by Rodrigo Duterte in 2016, whose term was marked by a controversial drug war. Ferdinand Marcos Jr. was elected president in May 2022, receiving the largest popular vote since his father's ouster.

The Philippine Government is threatened by several groups, including those on the US Government's Foreign Terrorist Organization list. A decades-long struggle against ethnic Moro insurgencies led to peace accords with the Moro National Liberation Front and the Moro Islamic Liberation Front.

The New People's Army, a Maoist-inspired insurgency, continues to operate across the country. In 2017, armed forces combated an ISIS-East Asia siege in Marawi City, prompting Duterte to declare martial law in the region.

Duterte achieved a peace deal in 2019 with the Moro Islamic Liberation Front, establishing the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao. Additionally, the Philippines faces increased tension with China over disputed territorial and maritime claims in the South China Sea.

Demographic profile

The Philippines is an ethnically diverse country in the early stages of demographic transition, with its fertility rate steadily declining since the 1950s. The decline accelerated after the introduction of a national population program in the 1970s1970s, largely due to increased use of modern contraceptive methods.

The country's total fertility rate TFR dropped below 5 in the 1980sbelow 4 in the 1990s, and below 3 in the 2010s. Despite these decreases, TFR remains above the replacement level at 2.9, particularly among the poor, rural residents, and the less-educated.

Elevated TFR is significantly due to the desire for more than two children, partly because children are seen as a means of financial assistance and security for aging parents, especially among the poor.
